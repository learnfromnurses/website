Alcohol info

Pregnant women are highly advised not to consume alcohol while they are pregnant.

In terms of social connectedness and relaxation, alcohol has played a long-standing role in culture. This includes beer, wine, wine coolers, and liquor. Overconsumption, on the other hand, has been linked to many major health and social issues. Alcohol use in pregnancy is still widespread, with the global prevalence of alcohol consumption in pregnancy estimated to be 9.8% from 1984 to 2014, with rates changing depending on where the woman resides (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Approximately 80% of women agree to drinking alcohol prior to being pregnant, 50% admit to drinking early in pregnancy before becoming pregnant, and 18% admit to drinking alcohol periodically throughout their pregnancy. Importantly, 11% admit to binge drinking throughout the first trimester of their pregnancy. Seventy percent of mothers who continued to drink during pregnancy reduced their intake to less than one unit per week (Khalil et al., 2010).

Alcohol crosses the placenta quickly, when a pregnant woman drinks, the alcohol passes through her bloodstream and into the blood, tissues, and organs of the baby in just 2 hours. In a baby's body, alcohol breaks down far more slowly than in an adult's. That indicates the baby's blood alcohol level is higher than the mother's for a longer period of time. Alcohol is a known teratogen that can affect fetal development and growth at any stage of pregnancy. This can be harmful to the newborn and can sometimes result in long-term consequences. As a result, there is no such thing as a "safe" level of alcohol consumption during pregnancy. According to some research, even small doses of alcohol might have the same negative impact on the fetus as binge drinking (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Chemical Caffeine


WHAT IS CAFFEINE?

Caffeine is a naturally occurring stimulant that is extensively taken across the globe. It helps you remain alert and may help you avoid feeling fatigued (Lieberman et. al., 1987).

HOW IT WORKS?
Caffeine works by activating the central nervous system, the heart, the muscles, and the blood pressure-regulating regions. Caffeine can elevate blood pressure but may not do so in chronic caffeine users. Caffeine can also function as a diuretic, increasing urine output. Caffeine is most frequently used for mental alertness, headaches, migraines, athletic performance, memory, and weight loss. Additionally, it is used to treat asthma, gallbladder disease, ADHD, hypotension, and depression, among other diseases (Grosso, 2017).

Chemical Drugs



Drugs info

Drugs are chemical molecules that have a psychoactive effect on the brain and body. The precise effects differ across individuals and also depend on the medicine, the dose, and the route of administration. Once a drug addiction takes place, it becomes the primary focus of a person's life. If you are hooked on narcotics, you will spend more time and money acquiring and utilizing them. This implies that you will have less time to engage in things you formerly enjoyed, such as sports and hobbies (Eske, 2020).

Occasionally, medicines are necessary for the pregnant woman's and fetus's health. In such circumstances, a woman should discuss the risks and advantages of the medicine with her doctor or other health care practitioner. A pregnant woman should consult her health care practitioner before taking any medication (including over-the-counter medications) or dietary supplement (including medicinal herbs).

Usage of illegal substances or misuse of prescription medications during pregnancy might have serious health effects for their infants. This is because many drugs readily pass through the placenta, which means that substances taken by a pregnant woman reach the fetus. Recent evidence indicates that consuming prescription pain medications or illicit narcotics during pregnancy increases the chance of stillbirth by a factor of two or even three. Regular use of some medicines can result in neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS), in which the newborn undergoes withdrawal (National institute on Drug Abuse, 2021).

In the United States, drug use among pregnant women is a rapidly growing problem. 4% of pregnant women take illicit drugs. As a result, 176,200 infants are at risk of developing birth abnormalities. These infants are at danger of preterm delivery, learning impairments, brain damage, HIV infection, drug addiction, delayed development, and birth deformities (12 keys, 2019).

In 2013 and 2014, marijuana had the highest rate among pregnant women who use illicit drugs in the United States. This is followed by cocaine and other illicit drugs having an average number of 90,000 and 45,000 pregnant women users. At some point during pregnancy, more than 50% of pregnant women consume prescription or nonprescription (over-the-counter) medicines, social drugs (such as cigarettes and alcohol), or illegal drugs, and drug use during pregnancy is growing. In general, medicines should be avoided during pregnancy unless absolutely essential, as many of them can be harmful to the fetus. Fewer than 2% to 3% of all birth abnormalities are caused by medicines given to treat a condition or symptom.

Chemical Smoke


WHAT IS SMOKING AND TOBACCO?

Tobacco is manufactured from the tobacco plant's leaves. It includes nicotine, a very addictive substance. When you smoke tobacco in the form of cigarettes, cigars, or pipes, you absorb harmful and carcinogenic compounds that have a harmful effect on your health (Henningfield, 2019).

WHAT IS SMOKING?
It is an inhalation and exhalation of gasses produced by burning plant material. While several plant materials, including marijuana and hashish, are inhaled, the act is most often associated with tobacco, whether in the form of a cigar, cigarette, or pipes (Henningfield, 2019).
