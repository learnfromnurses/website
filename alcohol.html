Substance Abuse in Pregnancy

Alcohol Abuse



Alcohol info

Pregnant women are highly advised not to consume alcohol while they are pregnant. In terms of social connectedness and relaxation, alcohol has played a long-standing role in culture. This includes beer, wine, wine coolers, and liquor. Overconsumption, on the other hand, has been linked to many major health and social issues. Alcohol use in pregnancy is still widespread, with the global prevalence of alcohol consumption in pregnancy estimated to be 9.8% from 1984 to 2014, with rates changing depending on where the woman resides (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Approximately 80% of women agree to drinking alcohol prior to being pregnant, 50% admit to drinking early in pregnancy before becoming pregnant, and 18% admit to drinking alcohol periodically throughout their pregnancy. Importantly, 11% admit to binge drinking throughout the first trimester of their pregnancy. Seventy percent of mothers who continued to drink during pregnancy reduced their intake to less than one unit per week (Khalil et al., 2010).

Alcohol crosses the placenta quickly, when a pregnant woman drinks, the alcohol passes through her bloodstream and into the blood, tissues, and organs of the baby in just 2 hours. In a baby's body, alcohol breaks down far more slowly than in an adult's. That indicates the baby's blood alcohol level is higher than the mother's for a longer period of time. Alcohol is a known teratogen that can affect fetal development and growth at any stage of pregnancy. This can be harmful to the newborn and can sometimes result in long-term consequences. As a result, there is no such thing as a "safe" level of alcohol consumption during pregnancy. According to some research, even small doses of alcohol might have the same negative impact on the fetus as binge drinking (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Negative Effects to Mothers

Excessive drinking is differently characterized by which factor group they belong to. And for our pregnant mothers, once they drink even any amount of alcohol that would be categorized as that (CDC, 2019). And so, there will always be some risk and negative effects to the mother. As the list follows;

Mood and Behavior Changes

The change of Mood and Behavior can be related to the current situation and feelings of the pregnant mother, but to the excessive drinking of the mother can cause certain changes or to worsen the current problem. From Vomiting, Sleepiness (CRC, 2020), and negative urgency (VanderVeen, et al. 2016), but as to longer periods of time might become agitation, anxiousness, depression, dependency and problem towards remission (Crum, et al., 2018).

Heart Problems

Excessive drinking will eventually cause heart problems to mothers. And such relating to the heart, are all fatal to the patient. The heart problems are:
● Cardiomyopathy
● Arrhythmias
● Hemorrhagic Stroke









Liver Problems

Alcohol Abuse are a major risk factor for certain liver problems, can cause the main stages of alcohol-related liver diseases, and such are:

● Steatosis/Fatty liver disease Stage
● Alcoholic Hepatitis Stage
● Fibrosis
● Cirrhosis Stage

Possible Cancer

The more damage the drinking cause on the part of the body, the more chances and increasing the risk of cancer. These can vary from Oral, pharynx/larynx, colorectal, esophageal, liver or breast cancers (Connor, 2016).

Additional info

Heart and Lung Problems

Cardiomyopathy (heart)

This is when the heart is having a hard time to pump blood to the rest of the body, this can be associated with alcohol abuse (Maisch, 2016).

Arrhythmias (heart)

This is when there is an irregular heartbeat, when there is an issue on the rate or rhythm of the heart. This is either based on the amount of alcohol intake, depending to any preexisting cardiovascular diseases or any heart failure that will affect the outcome (Pfeiffer, 2016).

Hemorrhagic Stroke (heart)

This is when a bleeding occurs into the brain, and so the stroke will happen. An increased risk for the Hemorrhagic stroke is based on the intake-dependent of the patient, as sudden elevations of blood pressure (Larsson, 2016).

Steatosis/Fatty liver disease Stage (lungs)

This can happen when there is a build-up of fats in the liver, and such drinking alcohol will cause it (Aberg, 2019).

Alcoholic Hepatitis Stage (lungs)

This is unrelated to the infectious hepatitis, and isn’t contagious. This can happen if there is alcohol misuse and such swelling of the liver and others may occur (Hosseini, 2019).

Fibrosis (lungs)

This can happen when there is less than normal blood flow throughout the liver, scarring occurs, and this is intake-dependent as it progressively worsen (Lackner, 2018).

Cirrhosis Stage (lungs)

This is the late stage of scarring of the liver caused by certain diseases and conditions, as this includes chronic alcoholism (Roerecke, et al., 2019).

Negative Effects to Fetus

When a pregnant mother consumes alcohol, the fetus does as well. Any quantity of alcohol taken throughout pregnancy may cause developmental abnormalities. (Grinfeld, 2000). According to a study, alcohol-dependent mothers, before and during their pregnancy, may result in fetal development anomalies (Bingham, 2015). The likelihood of a newborn being affected and the severity of the disorder are determined by the mother's alcohol intake, her drinking habits, mother and fetus blood alcohol levels and metabolism, women's health, the gestational age at which the fetus is exposed, and the fetus' genetic disposition. (Hannigan & Armant, 2000).

Alcohol may result in fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASDs). A baby may have one or more FASD includes (Thackray & Tifft, 2001):

1. Poor physical growth
2. Low body weight
3. Small head size
4. Shorter-than-average height

Alcohol Related Birth defects (ARBS) (Hoyme et al., 2005; Jones & Bass, 2003):
1. Fetal heart problems (atrial and ventricular problems, trouble in valve formation, and higher risk of developing heart diseases later on in life
2. Vision and hearing loss are also linked to this condition.
3. Bone and limb deformities.

Behavioral irregularities (Thackray & Tifft, 2001):
1. Hyperactive behavior
2. Poor attention and concentration skill
3. Impulsiveness, difficulty to control self
4. Newborns are fuzzy and jittery
5. difficulty getting along with their peers

Neurologic issues (Major & West, 2001; Thackray & Tifft, 2001):
1. Chance of Seizures
2. Impaired learning ability
3. Speech and language delays for their age
4. Poor reasoning and judgement skills
5. Intellectually challenged
6. Impaired memory
7. Poor problem-solving skills
8. Impaired reading
9. Impaired balance and coordination

When a neonate is abruptly removed from an alcohol-altering intrauterine environment due to medical complications, alcohol abstinence syndrome occurs, this may manifest as (Jones & Bass, 2003; Thackray & Tifft, 2001):
1. Irritability
2. Hyperexcitability and hypersensitivity to outside stimuli
Hypotonia or newborns appear to be limp and weak
3. Tremors
4. Muscle tension with opisthotonos that happens because of muscle spasms and unnatural posture in which the back becomes severely curved
5. Disturbed sleeping patterns
6. Frequent states of alert
7. Excessive signs of sweating
8. Apnea or a breathing condition is interrupted while sleeping
9. Tachypnea, another breathing condition where neonates breath fast

There is little documentation that investigates what is the specific amount of alcohol ingested that can cause complications in fetal growth. That is why pregnant women are advised to not consume any amount of alcohol during the whole period of pregnancy because there is no known safe portion about this issue (Pillitteri, 2017).

Tips to Avoid Alcohol

while Pregnant

Stopping drinking alcohol can be difficult or even dangerous, especially if you've had the habit for a long period or are frequently in social situations where others are drinking. Here are some suggestions for avoiding alcohol consumption:

1. Think ahead and plan alternate things to occupy the time, have fun, or relax if you're prone to drinking alcohol at certain times of the day.
2. Take a stroll, do prenatal yoga, or swim to get some exercise.
3. Self-care, such as meditation, should be a priority.
4. Switch to drinking water, fruity drinks, flavor water with flavor enhancers, and sparkling waters.
5. Avoid social events where people are likely to be drinking alcohol.
6. Remove any alcohol from your home and ask your family and friends to join you in your efforts by refusing to bring alcohol into your home.
7. Join an alcoholism support group.
8. Consult a doctor or therapist for assistance.

A lot of pregnant women believe the amount of alcohol they consume is safe. They may live or interact with individuals who use similar amounts of alcohol and think it "normal," ignorant that any amount of alcohol, regardless of kind, puts the fetus at risk (Slaughtery, 2020).

If you're worried about a friend or loved one who is pregnant or trying to get pregnant and you're worried about her drinking, talk to her. Avoid labelling her "alcoholic" and be empathetic when delivering your message. Give her the facts you've learned and encourage her to consult her doctor or a healthcare practitioner. Pregnancy is a powerful motivator for women to improve their drinking habits, and studies show that even brief behavioral counseling sessions with women who drink at-risk can reduce the likelihood of an alcohol-exposed pregnancy. Encourage your loved one to join a mutual-help organization or seek treatment if more aid is needed.
