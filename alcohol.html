Alcohol info

Pregnant women are highly advised not to consume alcohol while they are pregnant.

In terms of social connectedness and relaxation, alcohol has played a long-standing role in culture. This includes beer, wine, wine coolers, and liquor. Overconsumption, on the other hand, has been linked to many major health and social issues. Alcohol use in pregnancy is still widespread, with the global prevalence of alcohol consumption in pregnancy estimated to be 9.8% from 1984 to 2014, with rates changing depending on where the woman resides (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Approximately 80% of women agree to drinking alcohol prior to being pregnant, 50% admit to drinking early in pregnancy before becoming pregnant, and 18% admit to drinking alcohol periodically throughout their pregnancy. Importantly, 11% admit to binge drinking throughout the first trimester of their pregnancy. Seventy percent of mothers who continued to drink during pregnancy reduced their intake to less than one unit per week (Khalil et al., 2010).

Alcohol crosses the placenta quickly, when a pregnant woman drinks, the alcohol passes through her bloodstream and into the blood, tissues, and organs of the baby in just 2 hours. In a baby's body, alcohol breaks down far more slowly than in an adult's. That indicates the baby's blood alcohol level is higher than the mother's for a longer period of time. Alcohol is a known teratogen that can affect fetal development and growth at any stage of pregnancy. This can be harmful to the newborn and can sometimes result in long-term consequences. As a result, there is no such thing as a "safe" level of alcohol consumption during pregnancy. According to some research, even small doses of alcohol might have the same negative impact on the fetus as binge drinking (Dejong et.al., 2020).

Effects of

Drinking Alcohol

Excessive drinking is differently characterized by which factor group they belong to. And for our pregnant mothers, once they drink even any amount of alcohol that would be categorized as that (CDC, 2019). And so, there will always be some risk and negative effects to the mother. As the list follows;

Mood and Behavior Changes

The change of Mood and Behavior can be related to the current situation and feelings of the pregnant mother, but to the excessive drinking of the mother can cause certain changes or to worsen the current problem. From Vomiting, Sleepiness (CRC, 2020), and negative urgency (VanderVeen, et al. 2016), but as to longer periods of time might become agitation, anxiousness, depression, dependency and problem towards remission (Crum, et al., 2018).

Heart Problems

Excessive drinking will eventually cause heart problems to mothers. And such relating to the heart, are all fatal to the patient. The heart problems are:
● Cardiomyopathy
● Arrhythmias
● Hemorrhagic Stroke









Liver Problems

Alcohol Abuse are a major risk factor for certain liver problems, can cause the main stages of alcohol-related liver diseases, and such are:

● Steatosis/Fatty liver disease Stage
● Alcoholic Hepatitis Stage
● Fibrosis
● Cirrhosis Stage

Possible Cancer

The more damage the drinking cause on the part of the body, the more chances and increasing the risk of cancer. These can vary from Oral, pharynx/larynx, colorectal, esophageal, liver or breast cancers (Connor, 2016).

Additional info

Heart and Lung Problems

Cardiomyopathy (heart)

This is when the heart is having a hard time to pump blood to the rest of the body, this can be associated with alcohol abuse (Maisch, 2016).

Arrhythmias (heart)

This is when there is an irregular heartbeat, when there is an issue on the rate or rhythm of the heart. This is either based on the amount of alcohol intake, depending to any preexisting cardiovascular diseases or any heart failure that will affect the outcome (Pfeiffer, 2016).

Hemorrhagic Stroke (heart)

This is when a bleeding occurs into the brain, and so the stroke will happen. An increased risk for the Hemorrhagic stroke is based on the intake-dependent of the patient, as sudden elevations of blood pressure (Larsson, 2016).

Steatosis/Fatty liver disease Stage (lungs)

This can happen when there is a build-up of fats in the liver, and such drinking alcohol will cause it (Aberg, 2019).

Alcoholic Hepatitis Stage (lungs)

This is unrelated to the infectious hepatitis, and isn’t contagious. This can happen if there is alcohol misuse and such swelling of the liver and others may occur (Hosseini, 2019).

Fibrosis (lungs)

This can happen when there is less than normal blood flow throughout the liver, scarring occurs, and this is intake-dependent as it progressively worsen (Lackner, 2018).

Cirrhosis Stage (lungs)

This is the late stage of scarring of the liver caused by certain diseases and conditions, as this includes chronic alcoholism (Roerecke, et al., 2019).
