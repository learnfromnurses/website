SUBSTANCE ABUSE IN PREGNANCY

Chemical Drugs



Drugs info

Drugs are chemical molecules that have a psychoactive effect on the brain and body. The precise effects differ across individuals and also depend on the medicine, the dose, and the route of administration. Once a drug addiction takes place, it becomes the primary focus of a person's life. If you are hooked on narcotics, you will spend more time and money acquiring and utilizing them. This implies that you will have less time to engage in things you formerly enjoyed, such as sports and hobbies (Eske, 2020).

Occasionally, medicines are necessary for the pregnant woman's and fetus's health. In such circumstances, a woman should discuss the risks and advantages of the medicine with her doctor or other health care practitioner. A pregnant woman should consult her health care practitioner before taking any medication (including over-the-counter medications) or dietary supplement (including medicinal herbs).

Usage of illegal substances or misuse of prescription medications during pregnancy might have serious health effects for their infants. This is because many drugs readily pass through the placenta, which means that substances taken by a pregnant woman reach the fetus. Recent evidence indicates that consuming prescription pain medications or illicit narcotics during pregnancy increases the chance of stillbirth by a factor of two or even three. Regular use of some medicines can result in neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS), in which the newborn undergoes withdrawal (National institute on Drug Abuse, 2021).

In the United States, drug use among pregnant women is a rapidly growing problem. 4% of pregnant women take illicit drugs. As a result, 176,200 infants are at risk of developing birth abnormalities. These infants are at danger of preterm delivery, learning impairments, brain damage, HIV infection, drug addiction, delayed development, and birth deformities (12 keys, 2019).

In 2013 and 2014, marijuana had the highest rate among pregnant women who use illicit drugs in the United States. This is followed by cocaine and other illicit drugs having an average number of 90,000 and 45,000 pregnant women users. At some point during pregnancy, more than 50% of pregnant women consume prescription or nonprescription (over-the-counter) medicines, social drugs (such as cigarettes and alcohol), or illegal drugs, and drug use during pregnancy is growing. In general, medicines should be avoided during pregnancy unless absolutely essential, as many of them can be harmful to the fetus. Fewer than 2% to 3% of all birth abnormalities are caused by medicines given to treat a condition or symptom.

About cocaine

Cocaine is a Schedule II substance that is a strong stimulant with a high potential for abuse. Despite the fact that pharmaceutical cocaine is still used as a local anesthetic and vasoconstrictor, the bulk of cocaine is used illegally. Cocaine and other stimulants can be used to induce euphoria and increase a person's self-esteem. Some people take stimulants, including cocaine, to improve their mental and physical performance while also suppressing their hunger.

Drugs : Amphetamines

Amphetamines are stimulant medications, which accelerates the transmission of signals between the brain and body. Physicians prescribe some forms of amphetamines to treat illnesses such as attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), narcolepsy, and have also been utilized in the treatment of Parkinson's disease. Other kinds of amphetamines, such as speed, are illicitly manufactured and sold. Amphetamines have also been used to increase athletic performance. Crystal methamphetamine is the most potent type (ice). Generally, amphetamines are ingested, injected, smoked, or snorted.

Amphetamines are available in a variety of forms, from powder and tablets to crystals and capsules. When sold illegally, they may be packed in 'foils' (aluminum foil), plastic bags, or tiny balloons. Amphetamine powder ranges in color from white to brown, and occasionally contains traces of grey or pink. It has a pungent aroma and a bitter flavor. Capsules and tablets containing amphetamines vary significantly in size and color. Illegally manufactured amphetamines may contain a combination of pharmaceuticals, binding agents, caffeine, and sugar, as well as additional psychoactive chemicals.

Drugs : MARIJUANA AND HASHISH

Marijuana derives from the Hemp plant known as Cannabis Sativa. The main function, of the mind-altering chemical in marijuana, is tetrahydrocannabinol (THC), which targets the central nervous system. The present marijuana is reportedly 7 to 10 times more concentrated than marijuana used in the 1960s. Marijuana tends to be the first illegal and banned drug used by teenagers after tobacco and alcohol and it is considered a “gateway drug” as well as a primary addictive substance. Marijuana varies from all other common drugs of abuse in that it stays in the body far longer (up to 150 times as long as alcohol!) due to being fat-based instead of water-based. This “half-life” causes various negative effects.

Effects:
Feelings of calm and relaxation, the heart and pulse rate increases, drowsiness/sleepiness, bloodshot eyes, paranoia, decrease in memory and coordination, Amotivational Syndrome (is a loss of motivation and interest in life), lung cancer, bronchitis, emphysema, infertility for both men and women, decrease in school and work performance, tolerance, withdrawal, and addiction.

Drugs : INHALANTS

Inhalants are ubiquitous household, industrial, and medicinal substances that emit vapours that some people inhale (breathe in) to induce euphoria or to make them feel intoxicated. They are also referred to as glue, gasoline, sniff, huff, chroming, or poppers. Through the nose or mouth, inhalants are inhaled; t hey can be sprayed into a plastic bag, poured into a bottle, or absorbed into a fabric or sleeve prior to inhalation. Sometimes, they are immediately inhaled from the container or sprayed straight into the mouth or nose but this practice is particularly risky because it has the potential to result in suffocation.

Drugs : NARCOTIC AGONISTS

Narcotic agonists or opioid agonists are drugs used in relieving pain, such as morphine and meperidine, or suppressing cough such as codeine. These medications can be beneficial when taken carefully and under the supervision of a health care physician. However, misusing prescribed opioids can lead to dependency and addiction. (Prevent Opioid Abuse and Addiction, n.d.). These drugs produce potent analgesic or pain relief as well as the euphoric effect thus the reason why they are widely abused.

Based on statistics done by the National Center for Drug Abuse Statistics (NCDAS), with 136 deaths each day and rising, the opioid crisis is deemed a public health emergency by the Department of Health and Human Services. NCDAS reported that every year, about 50,000 people die as a result of an opioid overdose. Also, in a given year, almost ten million people abuse opioids and opioids are involved in at least seven out of ten overdose deaths, which is a total of 72%. (Opioid Epidemic, n.d.).

The Centers for Disease Prevention outlines the rise in opioid overdose deaths in three distinct waves. The first wave began in the 1990s with increased opioid prescribing. Prescription opioids, including natural and semi-synthetic opioids, as well as methadone, overdose deaths have been on the rise since at least 1999. With a sharp increase in heroin overdose deaths, the second wave occurred in 2010. The third wave started in 2013, with large rises in synthetic opioid overdose deaths, especially those containing illicitly made fentanyl (Understanding the Epidemic, n.d.).

The main opiate recreationally used to the extent of dependence is the drug called Heroin. Heroin, which is made from morphine, is a highly addictive substance that works as a depressant that inhibits the central nervous system (Buddy, 2021). It is a short-acting narcotic that produces an instant and momentary euphoric effect followed by sedation, which may be administered intradermally, intravenously, or through inhalation.
