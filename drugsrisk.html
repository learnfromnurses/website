SUBSTANCE ABUSE IN PREGNANCY

Chemical Drugs



Cocaine

Risks to the Mother

● migraines and seizures in the mother
● rupture of the membrane too soon
● separation of the placental lining from the uterus prior to delivery.

Cocaine usage exacerbates the usual circulatory changes that occur during pregnancy, which can lead to major difficulties with:
● hypertension (high blood pressure)
● spontaneous miscarriage
● premature labor
● premature labor

Cocaine

Risks to the Child

● preterm birth
● have low birth weights
● Head circumferences that are smaller
● are shorter in length than babies born to mothers who do not use cocaine.
● abruption of the placenta

Cocaine

Treatment and Management

● Seek expert assistance - a professional knows more than anybody else. Seeking professional treatment from a doctor, licensed psychologist, psychiatrist, mental health worker, or counselor after admitting you have a problem and desire to conquer your addiction is a critical step on the road to recovery. They will be able to analyze your condition and, if required, propose other treatment alternatives.

● Look for a residential treatment facility - If outpatient counseling and treatment aren't enough, a residential treatment program will not only provide you with the support of your peers and counselors, but will also keep you away from any situations that can trigger you to use cocaine.

● Join groups that provide assistance - peer support organizations, such as Cocaine Anonymous or Narcotics Anonymous, can provide a secure, comfortable environment for you to share your thoughts, feelings, and actions with others who are going through the same thing and can relate, whether in person or online.

● Medication - There are currently no FDA-approved medications for the treatment of cocaine use disorder or addiction. Certain antidepressants and stimulants, as well as other medications, may be administered to aid with cravings, withdrawal symptoms, or even to negate the positive effects of cocaine use.

Amphetamines

Risk to the Mother

● Mothers who are known to be using amphetamines are at increased risk for mental co-morbidity and worse obstetric outcomes, but their babies may go undetected since the indications of withdrawal are often less prominent than those seen in opiate-exposed newborns.

Amphetamines

Risk to the Child

● Infants exposed to amphetamines during pregnancy have been shown to suffer clefting, heart abnormalities, and fetal growth reduction impairments. Because exposed children may be at risk for long-term developmental and behavioral impairment, the authors advocate increasing the availability of early-intervention programs for affected children and their families.

Amphetamines

Treatment and Management

The first step in treatment is acknowledging that there is a problem. Once you've decided that you want to do something about your drug usage, the next step is to seek assistance and assistance.
Counseling is used in treatment programs to help patients improve their behavior (talk therapy). The goal is to assist you in better understanding your behaviors and the reasons for your use of amphetamines. Involving family and friends throughout therapy might help you feel supported and prevent you from relapsing into substance abuse.
If you are experiencing severe withdrawal symptoms, you may be required to remain in a residential treatment facility. As you recuperate, your health and safety may be monitored in that facility. Amphetamine usage can be reduced by inhibiting the effects of amphetamines, however at this time, there is no medication that can assist with this.








Marijuana and Hashish

Risk to the Mother

The risk of marijuana to the mother are the following:
● decreased blood pressure
● increased heart rate
● dizziness and falls
● increase the risk of anemia
● confusion
● forgetfulness during pregnancy

Marijuana and Hashish

Risk to the Child

● Fetal growth restriction (when a baby doesn’t gain the appropriate amount of weight before birth).
● A greater risk of stillbirth
● Preterm birth (being born before 37 weeks of gestation)
● Low birth weight
● Long-term brain development issues affecting memory, learning, and behavior.

Marijuana and Hashish

Treatment and Management

Pregnant women who have been discovered as marijuana users should be assigned for psychological therapy, detoxification, and psychiatric treatment. Neonates who have been exposed to marijuana must be tested for other substances and treated accordingly. Marijuana and tobacco use rarely require medical intervention. Prolonged exposure to medications like opioids may need a prolonged hospitalization and therapy of neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome.

Phencyclidine

Risk to the Mother

● High blood pressure
● Elevated heart rate
● Profuse sweating
● Flushed skin
● Numbness in the extremities
● Inability to feel pain
● Blurred vision
● Difficulty speaking
● Uncontrolled eye movement
● Lack of body control
● Rigid muscles
● A staring gaze
● Unconsciousness

Phencyclidine

Risk to the Child

● Jitteriness
● Hypertonicity
● Vomiting
● Diarrhea

Phencyclidine

Treatment and Management

A PCP overdose cannot be treated with any specific medications. Treatment is frequently adjusted to the individual's appearance. If a patient is aggressive, violent, confused, or delusional, sedative drugs (typically benzodiazepines) and restraints are frequently used. Until the person calms down, environmental stimuli should be maintained. This mainly involves turning off lights, reducing noise, and restricting visitors and medical workers to a bare minimum.
In order to administer some medications, the patient may have to be intubated if they are excessively hyperactive or anxious. After a person has been restrained, they can be administered intravenous fluids and activated charcoal, specifically when it is suspected that the person took the drug PCP orally. In instances of PCP overdose, activated charcoal has been shown to absorb PCP and reduce the risk of renal failure. Elevated body temperatures might necessitate the use of liquids and ventilation to cool them down.
The practitioners will keep an eye on the patient's vital signs and address any further symptoms that arise. Benzodiazepines can be used to manage seizure activity. Antipsychotic drugs may be utilized in certain circumstances if the patient's behavior is openly psychotic.





Inhalants

Risk to the Mother

During pregnancy:
● Preterm labor and delivery
● Respiratory problems
● Third trimester bleeding
● Toxemia
● Mortality

Inhalants include several poisonous compounds which are harmful to both the mother and the child. It is thought that practically all inhalants penetrate the placenta and enter the baby's bloodstream. For this reason, it is crucial you cease utilizing inhalants during pregnancy.

Inhalants

Risk to the Child

Research into this subject is sparse however some of the known impacts on the infant are:
● Low birthweight
● low growth during pregnancy
● low birth weight
● low growth in childhood
● decreased oxygen, which may influence brain growth.
● This has the ability to affect on a child's life long learning ability

Inhalants

Treatment and Management

There are only a restricted number of effective treatments for drug abuse during pregnancy, and these treatments are mostly based on behavioral therapy techniques.
Prenatal alcohol consumption has been demonstrated to be reduced by brief treatments particularly those that make use of motivational interviewing techniques. A recent randomized study with a telephone-based short intervention indicated that this way of reducing prenatal drinking may yield benefits that are comparable to those obtained with an in-person intervention method.
Some further strategies to minimize prenatal drinking that have recently been documented include screening by non-healthcare community workers, counseling by midwives and multimedia and educational activities aiming at raising awareness about the dangers of prenatal alcohol use.

Narcotic Agonists

Risks to the Mother

Because narcotics are usually injected through the use of shared needles, pregnancy complications of opiate-dependent women include the following:
According to Mayo Clinic (n.d.), Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) is easily transmitted by infected blood-contaminated needles and syringes, and is spread by the sharing of IV drug paraphernalia. Thus, people who share needles and syringes, like most opiate-dependent users, are at the greatest risk of acquiring Hepatitis B. The hepatitis B virus causes a dangerous liver infection that may manifest symptoms and signs that range from minor to severe, normally occurring one to four months after the person has been infected, though they might also appear as early as two weeks. According to the Cleveland Clinic (n.d.), symptoms of hepatitis B include the following:
● Mild fever
● Muscles or joint pain
● Stomach pain
● Constipation
● Loss of appetite
● Loose stool
● Brown urine
● Having yellow skin or eyes (jaundice)
● Being sick to the stomach
● Lack of energy

HIV INFECTION
The virus that causes Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) infection is easily transmitted by sharing or reusing dirty syringes, which most opiate-dependent users do. The human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is a virus that targets the immune system of the body, which may result in acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) if not treated (About HIV/AIDS, n.d.). According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (n.d.), possible symptoms of active HIV infection, which may last for several days or weeks, include the following:
● Fever
● Night sweats
● Chills
● Muscle aches
● Rash
● Fatigue
● Sore throat
● Mouth ulcers
● Swollen lymph nodes

PHLEBITIS
According to the study of Irish et al. (2007), intravenous injection can cause phlebitis or thrombophlebitis, which is an infection of the vein. Phlebitis refers to the inflammation of the vein which occurs due to blood clotting inside or damaged vein walls, which may manifest symptoms including redness of the skin and painful hard lumps underneath the skin (Phlebitis (superficial thrombophlebitis), n.d.).

SUBACUTE BACTERIAL ENDOCARDITIS
Subacute bacterial endocarditis is a kind of infective endocarditis that happens when bacteria or other organisms travel through the bloodstream and target the heart valve lining. It leads to growth in the heart valves, also known as vegetations, which damages the valves and spreads the infection outside of the heart and blood arteries (What Is Subacute Bacterial Endocarditis?, n.d.).

WITHDRAWAL SYMPTOMS As soon as six hours after the last drug dose and may continue for several days, withdrawal symptoms in opiate-dependent women may occur. The severity and duration of these symptoms are determined by the amount of drug used everyday, as well as the length of the dependence period. Withdrawal symptoms include the following:
● Nausea
● Vomiting
● Diarrhea
● Abdominal pain
● Hypertension
● Restlessness
● Shivering
● Insomnia
● Body aches
● Muscle jerks

Narcotic Agonists

Risk to the Child

● Fetal Opiate Dependence
Because drugs pass to babies from the placenta, babies of opiate-dependent women will also be dependent on the same drug.

● Severe Withdrawal Symptoms
The fetus will experience the same withdrawal symptoms as the mother would if she suddenly stopped taking the drug. These symptoms include: ○ Nausea
○ Vomiting
○ Diarrhea
○ Abdominal pain
○ Hypertension
○ Restlessness
○ Shivering
○ Insomnia
○ Body aches
○ Muscle jerks

● Small for gestational age
According to Liu et al. (2010), Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR) is a common occurrence in the pregnancies of opioid-dependent mothers (ODMs), and the causes are mostly unclear. Ratnam (2020) defined small for gestational age as a term used to describe a baby born with a lower weight and is smaller in size than other babies born at the same gestational age.

● Increased incidence of fetal distress and meconium aspiration
Fetal distress is an indication that something is wrong with the baby, which occurs when the placenta does not provide adequate oxygen to the infant (Fetal distress, n.d.). If left untreated, fetal stress and discomfort can result in meconium aspiration syndrome, wherein the baby breathes the meconium and amniotic fluid mixture into their lungs (Burke, 2018). Fetal distress may occur due to intrauterine growth retardation (IUGR), which is one of the risks to the fetuses of opiate-dependent mothers.

Narcotic Agonists

Treatment and Management

● METHADONE MAINTENANCE PROGRAM
According to Goff and O’Connor (2007) as mentioned by Pillitteri (2010), opiate-dependent women should be placed into a methadone maintenance program, which make use of methadone, a long-acting synthetic opioid analgesic, to treat Opioid Use Disorder (OUD). Because the said drug is an analgesic, meaning it is a pain reliever, during labor, or even postpartum, these women may require stronger analgesic prescription (Meyer, et al., 2007 as cited by Pillitteri, 2010). The advantage of this program is that it guarantees better nutrition of the fetus, better prenatal care, and less risk of acquiring hepatitis B and HIV. On the other hand, the disadvantage is that withdrawal symptoms of infants at birth would still occur. Unfortunately, some children born to women who were on methadone experienced more severe withdrawal symptoms than those born to women who were on heroin.

● BUPRENORPHINE
According to Pillitteri (2010), the women may be managed with buprenorphine if a methadone treatment is not possible. A drug called Suboxone, which is a combination of buprenorphine and naloxone, can be used to treat Opioid Use Disorder (OUD).
